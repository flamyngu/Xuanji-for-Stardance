# The Star Gauge

The Star Gauge (璇璣圖) is a Chinese literary work of the early Tang dynasty, attributed to the poet Su Hui (蘇蕙, also 蘇若蘭 *Sū Ruòlán*). It is a complex composition that consists of a 29 × 29 grid of Chinese characters, which can be read in various ways to produce a large number of poems. The work is known for its intricate structure and the ingenuity of its design, and it has been the subject of much scholarly interest and interpretation over the centuries. 

It is the first and most famous example of a "poem matrix" (詩矩陣), a genre of literary composition that involves arranging characters in a grid to create multiple poems through different reading paths. The Star Gauge is particularly notable for its use of palindromic structures and the way it allows for the generation of a vast number of poems from a single arrangement of characters. It is the first of its kind, and the most famous, and the most complex, and the most influential, and the most widely studied. But it still has not been fully translated into English, and thats what this project is about, I feel like it's the right thing to do, and I hope it will be a useful resource for scholars and readers interested in this fascinating work of Chinese literature.

Honestly, the Star Gauge is probably my most favourite piece of art I have ever laight my eyes upon and I am very excited to be working on this project. I hope to do it justice and to bring it to a wider audience through this translation(attempt).

# Primary sources

Most of this material reaches us through *Jìnghuā yuán* (《鏡花緣》, *Flowers in the Mirror*), the 1818 novel by Lǐ Rǔzhēn, which embedded Su Hui's diagram and its reading apparatus inside a chapter of fiction and so kept the whole package in circulation through the nineteenth and twentieth centuries. Without that novel, much of the operational knowledge of how to read the Star Gauge would not have survived.

I found four pages on ctext (https://ctext.org/) that together perserve the canonical *Xuán Jī Tú* (璇璣圖) material as it has been transmitted since the late Qing dynasty. Between them they hold the complete classical apparatus for the poem: the grid itself, Wǔ Zétiān's preface, the reading-methods key, and the biographical frame.

## 1. The biographical headnote and the 35-row diagram

- **URL:** https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=577197
- **Title:** 璇璣圖詩讀法 — *Methods of Reading Poems from the Xuanji Diagram*

The opening frame. Su Hui (蘇蕙, also 蘇若蘭 *Sū Ruòlán*, of Wǔgōng in Shǎnxī) under the Former Qín; the familiar story, her husband Dòu Tāo (竇滔) posted west, the concubine Zhào Yángtái (趙陽臺) taken with him to Xiāngyáng, Su Hui's refusal to follow, the eight-inch square of five-coloured brocade, the husband's eventual return and reconciliation.

This page carries the canonical figures: **over 800 characters**, **over 1,000 recoverable poems** in three-, four-, five-, six-, and seven-character meters. The diagram is appended in 35 rows as printed in *Jìnghuā yuán*. It also mentions the novel "Flowers in the Mirror", which we will get into eventually, hopefully...

## 2. The grid

- **URL:** https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=427301
- **Title:** 貫古妙作《璇璣圖》 — *A Wonderful Ancient Composition: The Star Gauge*

This page contains the bare grid, nothing more, nothing less.

## 3. The reading-methods key

- **URL:** https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=793483
- **Title:** 解讀璇璣圖 — *Interpreting the Star Gauge*

The systematic key to the classical reading methods:

- **Directional**: vertical, horizontal, diagonal
- **Rotational**: left rotation, right rotation
- **Colour-segmented**: the regional sub-poems marked by red, black, blue, and purple ink in the surviving editions — i.e. the Five-Phase regions, used as path-bounding constraints

More on the Reading Methods Key in **READING-METHODS.md**.

## 4. Wǔ Zétiān's preface

- **URL:** https://ctext.org/wiki.pl?if=en&chapter=655757
- **Title:** 璇璣圖詩讀法: 錄自《鏡花緣》 — *Recorded from* Flowers in the Mirror

The preface attributed to **大周天冊金輪皇帝** - Wǔ Zétiān, by her official Zhou-dynasty title, composed around 692 CE.

This is probably the single most influential document in the Star Gauge's reception history. It is the reason the poem entered the canon and stayed there. It establishes the canonical narrative: Su Hui, wife of Dòu Tāo, wove a brocade palindrome (織錦為回文) containing more than two hundred recoverable poems. The phrase that has anchored every subsequent reference to the work:

> 才情之妙，超古邁今
>
> *talents surpassing the ancient, exceeding the present.*
