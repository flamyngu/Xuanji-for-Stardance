**Full English translation:**

*(Kangxi: rolls 寅, lower; Shelter radical 广; p.351 #03)*

**Pronunciation.** *Tang Yun*, *Ji Yun*, *Yun Hui*: fanqie 古晃 — the rising tone of 光 (guǎng).

**Etymology and senses.** *Shuowen*: "the great roof of a hall." Also *Yupian*: "廣 means great (大)." *Guangyun*: "廣 means broad (闊)." Yijing, *Xici*: "broad and great (廣大), it matches Heaven and Earth"; subcommentary: "the great matches Heaven, the broad matches Earth."
- *Also a prefecture-name.* Tang shu, *Treatise on Geography*: "the Lingnan circuit has Guangzhou (廣州)."
- *Also a surname.* Xingpu: "from Danyang, descendants of Guangchengzi; the Song had Guang Han (廣漢)."
- *Also* *Yupian*: fanqie 古曠 — the departing tone of 光 (guàng). Zhouli, *Diguan, Da situ*: "to know fully the figures of the breadth and length (廣輪) of the lands of the Nine Provinces"; subcommentary: "Ma Rong says: east-west is 廣 (breadth), north-south is 輪 (length)"; Shiwen: "廣, fanqie 古曠."
- *Also a chariot-name.* Zhouli, *Chunguan, Chepu*: "the reserve of the broad-chariots (廣車)"; note: "the 廣車 is the chariot for ranging across in line"; Shiwen: "廣, fanqie 古曠." Zuozhuan, Xi yr 28: "the Western 廣 and the Eastern Palace guards"; subcommentary: "Chu had a Left and Right 廣 — names of war-chariot divisions." Xuan yr 2: "divided into two 廣"; note: "fifteen chariots make one 廣."
- *Also* *Zhengzitong*: pronounced 曠 (kuàng). Xunzi, *Wangba*: "why does the ruler not take his ease (廣)?"; note: "廣 is the look of being open and at ease."
- *Also interchangeable with 曠 (to leave idle).* Former Han, *Treatise on the Five Phases*: "when an army goes out past its season, this is called 廣 (idling)."
- *Also* fanqie 姑黃, sounding like 光 (guāng). Erya, *Shishou*: "a whorl of hair on the back — the quèguāng (闋廣)"; subcommentary: "in Bo Le's method of judging horses, one with a whorl of hair on the back is named quèguāng"; the Yinyi: "廣 is pronounced 光."
- *Rhymes* (per the *Yunbu*) as fanqie 果五, sounding like 古 (gǔ). Liji, *Yue ji*: "now the ancient music — advancing in rank and retiring in rank, harmonious, upright, and broad (廣); the strings, the gourd, the mouth-organ, and the reed wait together upon the hand-drum; it begins its performance with the civil and concludes its movement with the martial."
