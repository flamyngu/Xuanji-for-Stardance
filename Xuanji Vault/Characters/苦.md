**Full English translation:**

*(Kangxi: rolls з”і, upper; Grass radical иүё; p.1023 #16)*

**Pronunciation.** *Tang Yun*: fanqie еә·еңҹ. *Ji Yun*, *Yun Hui*: fanqie еӯ”дә” вҖ” sounding like дҮў (kЗ”).

**Etymology and senses.** *Shuowen*: "еӨ§иӢҰ (great bitter) is the *ling* [liquorice]." Shijing, *Tangfeng*: "I gather sowthistle, gather sowthistle (иӢҰ), below Shouyang"; tradition: "иӢҰ is the bitter herb (иӢҰиҸң)." Lu Ji says: "it grows in fields and on hills and marshes; after frost it turns sweet, crisp, and good." Xie Lingyun, *Shanju fu*: "two bamboos of differing leaf, four 'bitters' of like savour"; his own note: "the four bitters вҖ” green-bitter, white-bitter, purple-bitter, yellow-bitter."
- *A taste.* Shu, *Hongfan*: "[fire,] blazing upward, makes the bitter (иӢҰ)." Shijing, *Beifeng*: "who says the sowthistle is bitter (иӢҰ)? вҖ” it is sweet as shepherd's-purse."
- *To toil (еӢӨ).* *Ji Yun.* Mengzi: "[Heaven] must first make bitter (иӢҰ) his heart and will."
- *To suffer, be distressed by (жӮЈ).* Former Han, *Biography of Jia Yi*: "not only sick with swelling, but also afflicted (иӢҰ) by [a foot] sprained and twisted."
- *Urgent, pressing (жҖҘ).* *Leipian.*
- *Downcast, rueful (жӮө).* *Boya.*
- *Pleased, glad.* Yangzi's *Fangyan*: "glad"; in Chu they say иӢҰ, in Qin дәҶ. Guo Pu says: "'bitter' meaning 'glad' is like 'stink' for 'fragrant' or 'order' for 'disorder' вҖ” words used in reverse."
- *Departing tone.* *Zheng Yun*: fanqie иӢҰж•…, sounding like еә« (kГ№) вҖ” "distressed, hard-pressed." Xixi congyu: "people today who are poor sailors call it 'boat-sick (иӢҰиҲ№)'; northerners say 'cart-sick (иӢҰи»Ҡ).'"
- *A surname.* The grandee Kucheng; see the Guoyu. Also Tongzhi, *Monograph on Clans*: "the Han had Ku Zhuo, governor of Kuaiji."
- *Fanqie жһңдә”, sounding like еҸӨ (gЗ”) вҖ” "bad, of poor make."* Zhouli, *Dongguan kaogong ji*: "to tell its poor (иӢҰ) from its good"; note: "to distinguish the coarse from the fine." The Qiyu: "to tell its sound (еҠҹ) from its flimsy (иӢҰ)"; note: "еҠҹ is sturdy, иӢҰ is brittle." Shiji, *Annals of the Five Emperors*: "the wares from the banks of the River were none of them shoddy (иӢҰзӘі)"; note: "иӢҰ means coarse."
- *Sounding like жҖҷ (hГ№), a place-name.* Shiji, *Biographies of Laozi and Zhuangzi*: "Laozi was a man of Quren hamlet, Li village, Ku county (иӢҰзёЈ) in Chu."

**On the graph.** A note: in иӢҰиүҜ, иӢҰзӘі, and еҠҹиӢҰ, the иӢҰ is sometimes written жҘӣ or жІҪ, and is also interchangeable with зӣ¬, the sense being the same. The Zhiyin writes иӢҰ.
