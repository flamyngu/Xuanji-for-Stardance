**Full English translation:**

*(Kangxi: rolls 午, upper; Jade radical 玉; p.735 #11)*

**Ancient graphs.** 𨪖, 𨫹, 𤫍, 珡, 𩰔.

**Pronunciation.** *Tang Yun*: fanqie 巨今. *Ji Yun*, *Yun Hui*, *Zheng Yun*: fanqie 渠金 — sounding like 靲, i.e. **qín**.

**Etymology (Shuowen).** Originally written 珡. It means "to restrain" (禁 jìn); a pictograph. Made by Shennong: with a hollow sound-board (*dong-yue*), strings dyed cinnabar-red (*lian-zhu*), and five strings; the Zhou added two more. **Xu Kai**: "that by which the gentleman restrains himself." *Baihu Tong*: "the qin restrains licentiousness and depravity and rectifies the human heart."

**On the instrument (Qin Lun).** Fuxi pared paulownia wood into the qin: its rounded face models Heaven, its square base images Earth; the Dragon Pool, eight inches, communicates the eight winds; the Phoenix Pool, four inches, matches the four *qi*. The qin is three *chi* six *cun* long, imaging the 360 days; six *cun* broad, imaging the Six Directions; broad in front and narrow behind, imaging high and low rank; round above and square below, modelling Heaven and Earth. The five strings image the Five Phases — the great string is the ruler, the small string the minister; the two strings added by [Kings] Wen and Wu match the bond of ruler and minister.

**Parts of the qin (Sanli Tu).** The first string is *gong*, then *shang, jue, yu, zhi*, then lesser-*gong*, then lesser-*shang*. The qin has strings and studs (*hui*), head and tail, lips and feet, belly and back, waist, shoulders, and "*yue*." The lip is the "dragon-lip," the foot the "phoenix-foot," the back the "immortal," the waist the "fair lady." The long *yue* is the Dragon Pool, the short the Phoenix Pond. The *linyue* at the head and the *yueshan* (rising high) at the tail hold the strings taut; the "city road" is the channel beneath the *yueshan*; the "goose-feet" beneath the shoulders fasten the strings; the *zhen* (tuning pegs) beneath the feet are turned to tune them.

**Famed qins (Zhengzitong).** Names celebrated for their elegance: those of Fuxi; "Ying-bi" and "Gong-zi"; the qin the Thearch commanded Bo-huang to make, named "Dan-wei" and "Zu-chuang"; Dijun's qin "Dianmu" (Lightning-Mother); his son Yanlong's "Jun-shou" and "Bai-min"; Yi Zhi's "Guo-e"; King Xuan of Zhou's "Xiang-feng" (inscribed: "feeling has ears, the foe lies in ambush"); Zhu's qin "Tai-gu"; the Chu noble Wu-kui's "Qing-fan"; Cui Yin's "Wo-bing" (Lying on Ice); Dai Kui's "Hei-gu" (Black Swan); his son Zhong-ruo's "Yue-fang"; his brother Bo's "Ying-gu"; Liu Wenchang's "Chun-feng" (Spring Wind) — these may rank in ornament with the famed "Hao-zhong," "Lu-yue," "Lü-qi," and "Jiao-wei."

**Other senses.**
- *Place name.* Gongyang Zhuan, Duke Ding yr 8: "it arose at Qin-ru"; commentary: "Qin-ru, a place name."
- *A dialect verb.* Shanhaijing: between the Black Waters in the southwest lies the wild of Guangdu, where winter and summer they "*bo-qin*"; Guo Pu: "*bo-qin* is like 'to sow and plant' — a regional expression."
- *Surname.* Qin Zhang, a disciple of Confucius, styled Zikai (Zuozhuan, Zhao yr 20).
- *Personal name.* Shanhaijing: west of the Red Water lies the Land of the First People, with Mt. Mang, Mt. Gui, and Mt. Yao; upon it dwells one styled "Prince Long-qin (Tall Zither)."
- *Insect name.* Shanhaijing: in the land of the Sushen there is a creature, beast-headed and snake-bodied, called the "qin-insect"; Guo Pu: "also a kind of snake."
- *A tomb name.* The men of Chu call a barrow "qin." In Lu'an county, within the Du marsh, stands a great barrow the people call "Duke Qin," handed down as the tomb of Gao Yao (Shuijingzhu).
- *Wind-chimes.* The iron chimes before the eaves are called the "wind-qin"; with a poem by Wang Anshi.

**Textual note.** In the Shanhaijing passage, 瑤山 should read 榣山.
